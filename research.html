    Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

    The potential energy for a classical harmonic oscillator takes the form:

    $$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}kx^{2}$$

    Equivalently

    $$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^{2}x^{2}, \quad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}

    Thus, the Time-Independent Schrodinger Equation (TISE) reads:

    $$\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    and so we need to solve:

    $$\overbrace{\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2}}^{T(x)} + \overbrace{\frac{m\omega^2 x^2}{2}}^{V(x)}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    Recall:

    $$T(x) = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$$

    and $$p = mv$$, so

    $$T(x) = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2} = \frac{m^{2}v^{2}}{2m} = \frac{p^{2}}{2m}

    Transmon Hamiltonian

    The transmon is a nonlinear oscillator. Its Hamiltonian in the charge basis is:

    $$\hat{H} = 4E_C\hat{n}^2 - E_J\cos\hat{\varphi}$$

    where $E_C$ is the charging energy, $E_J$ is the Josephson energy, $\hat{n}$ is the Cooper pair number operator, and $\hat{\varphi}$ is the superconducting phase. The transmon regime is defined by $E_J / E_C \gg 1$, which exponentially suppresses charge dispersion while only polynomially reducing anharmonicity.

    In the Fock (harmonic oscillator) approximation, we expand the cosine to fourth order:

    $$\hat{H} \approx \sqrt{8E_JE_C}\left(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \frac{1}{2}\right) - \frac{E_C}{12}\left(\hat{a} + \hat{a}^\dagger\right)^4$$

    The first term gives us equispaced levels at frequency $\omega_q = \sqrt{8E_JE_C} - E_C$, and the quartic correction introduces the anharmonicity $\alpha \approx -E_C$ that makes the transmon usable as a qubit.
