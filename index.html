  • Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

    This a summary of chapter 2.3 of Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, by Griffiths

    The potential energy for a classical harmonic oscillator takes the form:

    $$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}kx^{2}$$

    Equivalently

    $$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^{2}x^{2}, \quad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$

    The Time-Independent Schrodinger Equation (TISE) in the position basis reads:

    $$\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    and so we need to solve:

    $$\overbrace{\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2}{dx^2}}^{T}\psi(x) + \overbrace{\frac{m\omega^2 x^2}{2}}^{V(x)}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    Recall:

    $$T = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$$

    and $$p = mv$$ so

    $$T = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2} = \frac{m^{2}v^{2}}{2m} = \frac{p^{2}}{2m}$$

    TISE now becomes:

    $$\frac{p^2}{2m}\psi(x) + \frac{m\omega^2 x^2}{2}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    $$\iff$$

    $$\overbrace{\frac{1}{2m}\left(p^2 + (m\omega x)^2\right)}^{\mathcal{H}}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    So we wish to factor the Hamiltonian as:

    $$\mathcal{H} = \frac{1}{2m}\left(p^2 + (m\omega x)^2\right)$$

    Classically, this would be easy, we simply recall:

    $$(u^2 + v^2) = (u + iv)(u - iv)$$

    But we have to quantize and upgrade to operators first:

    $$\hat{\mathcal{H}} = \frac{1}{2m}\left(\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega \hat{x})^2\right)$$

    and operators do not commute, nonetheless, we can define:

    $$\hat{a}_{\pm} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar m\omega}}\left(\mp i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}\right)$$

    Thus,

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}(i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x})(-i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}) \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[(i\hat{p})(-i\hat{p}) + (i\hat{p})(m\omega\hat{x}) + (m\omega\hat{x})(-i\hat{p}) + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2 - im\omega(\hat{x}\hat{p} - \hat{p}\hat{x})\right] \quad \leftarrow \text{commutator} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] - \frac{i}{2\hbar}[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] \end{aligned}$$

    Now, we know that $$\hat{x}$$ and $$\hat{p}$$ are conjugate variables, so by definition,

    $$[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$$

    and we can now rewrite our equation as

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] - \frac{i}{2\hbar}(i\hbar) \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] + \frac{1}{2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\overbrace{\left(\frac{1}{2m}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right]\right)}^{\hat{\mathcal{H}}} + \frac{1}{2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{\mathcal{H}} + \frac{1}{2} \quad \iff \quad \hat{\mathcal{H}} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ - \frac{1}{2}\right) \end{aligned}$$

    Similarly,

    $$\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- = \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{\mathcal{H}} - \frac{1}{2} \quad \iff \quad \hat{\mathcal{H}} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- + \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

    Thus,

    $$\hat{\mathcal{H}} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_{\pm}\hat{a}_{\mp} \pm \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

    SE now reads:

    $$\hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_{\pm}\hat{a}_{\mp} \pm \frac{1}{2}\right)\psi = E\psi$$

    $$\text{Lemma: } \hat{\mathcal{H}}\psi = E\psi \implies \hat{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{a}_+\psi) = (E + \hbar\omega)(\hat{a}_+\psi), \quad \hat{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{a}_-\psi) = (E - \hbar\omega)(\hat{a}_-\psi)$$

    $$\text{Lemma: } \hat{a}_+\psi_n = \sqrt{n+1}\;\psi_{n+1} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}_-\psi_n = \sqrt{n}\;\psi_{n-1}$$

    Also note,

    $$\hat{x} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}(\hat{a}_+ - \hat{a}_-) \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{p} = i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}(\hat{a}_+ - \hat{a}_-)$$

  • The Fock Basis

    First, it is important to note

    $$\psi = \ket{\psi}$$

    this object is the physical wavefunction in the abstract sense, i.e. in no particular basis.

    We can then write

    $$\begin{aligned} |\psi\rangle &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle \\ &\equiv \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n|n\rangle, \quad |n\rangle = \vec{e}_n \\ &= |\psi\rangle \\ \end{aligned}$$

    Note the congruence $\equiv$ denoting the act of projection.

    Now, let us build $\hat{a}$ and $\hat{a}^{\dagger}$ in the Fock basis.

    We know:

    $$\hat{a}\,\psi_n = \sqrt{n}\;\psi_{n-1} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}^\dagger\psi_n = \sqrt{n+1}\;\psi_{n+1}$$

    Equivalently:

    $$\hat{a}\ket{n} = \sqrt{n}\ket{n-1} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}^\dagger\ket{n} = \sqrt{n+1}\ket{n+1}$$

    $$\implies \bra{m}\hat{a}\ket{n} = \sqrt{n}\braket{m}{n-1} = \sqrt{n}\;\delta_{m,n-1}$$

    and

    $$\bra{m}\hat{a}^\dagger\ket{n} = \sqrt{n+1}\braket{m}{n+1} = \sqrt{n+1}\;\delta_{m,n+1}$$

    In the Fock basis,

    $$\hat{a} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \sqrt{1} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \sqrt{3} & \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \end{bmatrix}, \quad \hat{a}^\dagger \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ \sqrt{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \sqrt{3} & \vdots & \end{bmatrix}$$

    $$\text{Now let } \hat{n} = \hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 3 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix} = [n]_F$$

    Thus,

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 &= \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) \\ &= \hbar\omega\left(\hat{n} + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) \\ &\equiv \hbar\omega\left([n]_F + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{I}\right) \end{aligned}$$

    Which is then diagonal in the Fock basis

    We can now diagonalize $\hat{\mathcal{H}_0}$ to get:

    $$[\mathcal{H}_0]_E = [V][\mathcal{H}_0]_F[V^{-1}] = [V][\mathcal{H}_0]_F[V^\dagger]$$

    Since $\hat{\mathcal{H_0}}$ is Hermitian $\implies V \text{ unitary} \implies V^{-1} = V^\dagger$

    and the columns of $[V]$ are the energy eigenstates/vectors of $[\mathcal{H}_0]_E$ in the Fock basis

    In the case of a harmonic oscillator, $[\mathcal{H}_0]_F$ is diagonal so $[V] = \mathbb{I}$

    i.e the energy basis and Fock basis are identical.

  • Approximating an $RY(\theta)$ Unitary with a Harmonic Oscillator

    These are notes on Compact Pulse Schedules for High-Fidelity Single-Flux Quantum Qubit Control, by Shillito et al.

    Lets now set ncut = 3 and see how we might apply an RY gate

    Set our initial state

    $$\psi_0 = \ket{\psi_0} \equiv \sum_{n=1}^{3} c_n\ket{\psi_n} = \sum_{n=1}^{3} c_n\ket{n} = \vec{e}_1$$

    Define qubit period in seconds $T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$

    Define free evolution unitaries:

    $$\hat{U}(t)\ket{\psi} = \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 t/\hbar)\ket{\psi}$$

    let

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{U}_1 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_2 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{2T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_3 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{3T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_4 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{4T}{4})/\hbar) \end{aligned}$$

    Fix the kick angle defined by our system

    $$\theta = C_c \Phi_0 \sqrt{2\omega / C}, \quad \Phi_0 = \frac{h}{2e}$$

    w/ $C_c$ = capacitance between oscillator and SFQ driver

    Define the kick unitary operator

    $$\hat{U}_\theta = \exp(-\tfrac{\theta}{2}[\hat{a}^\dagger - \hat{a}])$$

    Then

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta) \approx \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta = \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta$$

    and

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{RY}(\theta_{\text{target}}) &\approx \hat{RY}(n\theta) \qquad (N = \theta_{\text{target}} / \theta) \\ &= \hat{RY}(\theta)^N \\ &\approx (\hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta)^N \\ &= (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^N \end{aligned}$$

    Let $\theta_{\text{target}} = \pi/2$ and $\theta = 0.03$ (typical) s.t $ = \frac{\pi}{2\theta} \approx 52$

    Also, let $C = m$ and $L = \tfrac{1}{k} s.t. $\omega = \sqrt{\tfrac{k}{m}} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} rad/sec$

    So

    $$\hat{RY}(\tfrac{\pi}{2}) = (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^{52}$$

    Lets apply the first kick, (in Fock basis)

    $$\begin{aligned} \ket{\psi_2} &= \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta\ket{\psi_0} \\ &\equiv [U_4]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}([a^\dagger] - [a])}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[V][D][V^{-1}]}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[D]}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]\begin{bmatrix} e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_3} \end{bmatrix}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]\ket{\psi}_{\text{1}} \quad \leftarrow \text{rotated a little more toward } \ket{1} \\ &= e^{-i[\mathcal{H}_0]T/\hbar}\ket{\psi_1}, \quad [\mathcal{H}_0] \text{ is diagonal in the Fock basis} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iT\hbar\omega(0+\frac{1}{2})/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iT\hbar\omega(1+\frac{1}{2})/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iT\hbar\omega(2+\frac{1}{2})/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iT\omega/2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-3iT\omega/2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-5iT\omega/2} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{\omega}\frac{\omega}{2}} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i\frac{3}{2}\frac{2\pi}{\omega}\omega} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-i\frac{5}{2}\frac{2\pi}{\omega}\omega} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-i\pi} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-3i\pi} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-5i\pi} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \\ &= -\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \quad \leftarrow \text{global phase} \end{aligned}$$

    The issue here stems from the fact that we are unable to address the computational subspace, those being the amplitudes corresponding to $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and inevitably interact with higher levels like that corresponding to $\gamma$, which would be $\ket{2}$ (bad).

  • The Transmon, an Anharmonic Quantum Oscillator

    Note that in a superconducting circuit,

    $$\text{Flux } \Phi \equiv \text{Position } x$$

    and

    $$\text{Charge } Q \equiv \text{Momentum } p$$

    in that

    $$[\Phi, Q] = i\hbar$$

    Also

    $$C \equiv m$$

    A transmon can be parameterized by a charging energy, $E_C$, as well as a ratio $\frac{E_J}{E_C} \gg 1$, where $E_J$ is the Josephson energy and

    $$E_C = \frac{e^2}{2C}$$

    The energy for the Josephson element in the phase basis reads:

    $$h_J(\Phi) = -E_J\cos\!\left(\frac{\Phi - \Phi_{\text{offset}}}{\Phi_0}\right), \quad \Phi_0 = \frac{\hbar}{2e}$$

    where $\varphi = \frac{\Phi}{\Phi_0}$ is the gauge-invariant phase difference, so equivalently:

    $$h_J(\varphi) = -E_J\cos(\varphi)$$

    In a superconducting circuit with a ground node electrode and an island electrode separated by a Josephson junction,

    $$n = \text{number of Cooper pairs that crossed the junction}$$

    and we know, in the charge basis:

    $$\hat{n} \equiv \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n|n\rangle\langle n|$$

    Lemma: $\Delta n = \pm 1$

    Define $\hat{T}^+$ such that

    $$\hat{T}^+|n\rangle = |n+1\rangle$$

    Now,

    $$\hat{T}^+ = \hat{T}^+\mathbb{I} = \hat{T}^+\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n| = \sum_n \hat{T}^+|n\rangle\langle n| = \sum_n |n+1\rangle\langle n|$$

    Then in the charge basis:

    $$\langle m|\hat{T}^+|n\rangle = \langle m|n+1\rangle = \delta_{m,n+1}$$

    Similarly, define $\hat{T}^-$ such that

    $$\hat{T}^-|n\rangle = |n-1\rangle \iff \hat{T}^- = \sum_n |n-1\rangle\langle n|$$

    Then:

    $$\langle m|\hat{T}^-|n\rangle = \delta_{m,n-1}$$

    Lemma: The coupling Hamiltonian reads:

    $$\hat{h}_{CPT} = -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[\hat{T}^- + \hat{T}^+\right]$$

    Now, in the charge basis, define the phase states:

    $$|\theta\rangle = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta}|n\rangle, \quad \theta \in [0, 2\pi)$$

    We want to show that:

    $$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta = |m\rangle$$

    So we expand,

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle &= \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta}|n\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    Integrate both sides:

    $$\begin{aligned} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta &= \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta}|n\rangle\,d\theta \\ &= \sum_n |n\rangle \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta \end{aligned}$$

    Case 1: $n = m$

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot 2\pi \\ &= 1 \end{aligned}$$

    Case 2: $n \neq m$

    $$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta = 0$$

    Therefore:

    $$\begin{aligned} \sum_n |n\rangle \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta &= \sum_n |n\rangle\,\delta_{n,m} \\ &= |m\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta = |m\rangle$$

    Now rewrite $\hat{h}_{CPT}$:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{h}_{CPT} &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[\hat{T}^- + \hat{T}^+\right] \\ &\equiv -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \left[|n-1\rangle\langle n| + |n+1\rangle\langle n|\right] \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \left[|n-1\rangle + |n+1\rangle\right]\langle n| \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \left[\left(\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-i(n-1)\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta\right) + \left(\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-i(n+1)\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta\right)\right]\left(\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{in\theta}\langle\theta|\,d\theta\right) \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\left(e^{-i(n-1)\theta} + e^{-i(n+1)\theta}\right)e^{in\theta}|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|\,d\theta \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}\left(e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}\right)|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|\,d\theta \end{aligned}$$

    Now define the phase operator:

    $$e^{i\hat{\theta}} \equiv \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\theta}|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|\,d\theta$$

    Then:

    $$e^{i\hat{\theta}}|n\rangle \equiv \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\theta}|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|n\rangle\,d\theta$$

    Using

    $$|\theta\rangle = \sum_n e^{in\theta}|n\rangle \iff \langle\theta| = \sum_m e^{-im\theta}\langle m|$$

    we get

    $$\langle\theta|n\rangle = e^{-in\theta}$$

    so:

    $$\begin{aligned} e^{i\hat{\theta}}|n\rangle &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\theta}|\theta\rangle e^{-in\theta}\,d\theta \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{-i\theta(n-1)}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta \\ &= |n-1\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    Thus:

    $$e^{i\hat{\theta}} = \hat{T}^+$$

    Similarly:

    $$e^{-i\hat{\theta}} = \hat{T}^-$$

    And so:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{h}_{CPT} &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[\hat{T}^- + \hat{T}^+\right] \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[e^{-i\hat{\theta}} + e^{i\hat{\theta}}\right] \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\cdot 2\cos(\hat{\theta}) \\ &= -E_J\cos(\hat{\theta}) \end{aligned}$$

    Let $\varphi = \theta \mod 2\pi$, then:

    $$\hat{h}_J \equiv h_J(\varphi) = -E_J\cos(\varphi) \equiv -E_J\cos(\hat{\varphi}) = \hat{h}_{CPT}$$

    This confirms that the Josephson cosine potential and the Cooper pair tunneling Hamiltonian are identical.

    The full transmon Hamiltonian is therefore:

    $$\hat{H} = 4E_C\hat{n}^2 - E_J\cos(\hat{\varphi})$$

    We know:

    $$e^{i\theta} = \cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta), \quad e^{-i\theta} = \cos(\theta) - i\sin(\theta)$$

    $$\implies \cos\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2}$$

    So $\hat{H}$ can also be written as

    $$\hat{H} = 4E_C\hat{n}^2 - E_J\left(\frac{e^{i\hat{\varphi}} + e^{-i\hat{\varphi}}}{2}\right)$$

    Lemma: $[\hat{n}, \hat{\varphi}] = -i \implies e^{i\hat{\varphi}}\ket{n} = \ket{n+1}$ and $e^{-i\hat{\varphi}}\ket{n} = \ket{n-1}$

    (which we have now proven above via the tunneling operators)

    Thus,

    $$\begin{aligned} -E_J\left(\frac{e^{i\hat{\varphi}} + e^{-i\hat{\varphi}}}{2}\right)\ket{n} &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left(e^{i\hat{\varphi}}\ket{n} + e^{-i\hat{\varphi}}\ket{n}\right) \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left(\ket{n+1} + \ket{n-1}\right) \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \bra{m}\left[-E_J\left(\frac{e^{i\hat{\varphi}} + e^{-i\hat{\varphi}}}{2}\right)\right]\ket{n} = -\frac{E_J}{2}\left(\braket{m}{n+1} + \braket{m}{n-1}\right)$$

    and since $\hat{n}$ is diagonal in the charge basis, we get

    $$\hat{H} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ -\frac{E_J}{2} & 4E_C & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 16E_C & -\frac{E_J}{2} & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 36E_C & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix} = [\hat{\mathcal{H}}]_{charge}$$

    Now diagonalize $\hat{H}$ in the charge basis:

    $$[\mathcal{H}]_{\text{charge}} = [V][\mathcal{H}]_E[V^\dagger]$$

    where $[\mathcal{H}]_E = \text{diag}(E_0, E_1, E_2, \ldots)$ and the columns of $[V]$ are the energy eigenstates in the charge basis.

    Define the anharmonicity:

    $$\alpha = (E_2 - E_1) - (E_1 - E_0)$$

    the qubit frequency:

    $$f_q = \frac{E_1 - E_0}{h}$$

    and the angular frequency:

    $$\omega_q = 2\pi f_q$$

    Note: unlike the harmonic oscillator, $[\mathcal{H}]_{\text{charge}}$ is not diagonal, so $[V] \neq \mathbb{I}$ — the energy basis and charge basis are distinct.

  • Approximating an $RY(\theta)$ Unitary with a Transmon

    First, note that the charge operator can be approximated in the Fock basis as follows:

    $$[\hat{n}]_F \approx ir(\hat{a}^{\dagger} - \hat{a}), \quad r = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{E_J/E_C}{2}\right)^{1/4}$$

    We will use $[\hat{n}]_F$ to build the kick unitary $\hat{U}_{\theta}$, as Shillito does in the paper.

    Lets once more set ncut = 3 and see how we might apply an RY gate

    Set our initial state

    $$\psi_0 = \ket{\psi_0} \equiv \sum_{n=1}^{3} c_n\ket{\psi_n} = \sum_{n=1}^{3} c_n\ket{n} = \vec{e}_1$$

    Define qubit period in seconds $T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_q}$

    Define free evolution unitaries:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{U}_1 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_2 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{2T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_3 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{3T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_4 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{4T}{4})/\hbar) \end{aligned}$$

    Fix the kick angle defined by our system

    $$\theta = C_c \Phi_0 \sqrt{2\omega_q / C}$$

    w/ $C_c$ = capacitance between oscillator and SFQ driver

    Define the kick unitary operator

    $$\hat{U}_\theta = \exp(\tfrac{\theta'}{2}\hat{n})$$

    where

    $$\theta' = \theta / r$$

    Then

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta) \approx \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta = \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta$$

    and

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{RY}(\theta_{\text{target}}) &\approx \hat{RY}(N\theta) \qquad (N = \theta_{\text{target}} / \theta) \\ &= \hat{RY}(\theta)^N \\ &\approx (\hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta)^N \\ &= (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^N \end{aligned}$$

    Let $\theta_{\text{target}} = \pi/2$ and $\theta = 0.03$ (typical) s.t $N = \frac{\pi}{2\theta} \approx 52$

    So

    $$\hat{RY}(\tfrac{\pi}{2}) = (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^{52}$$

    Lets apply the first kick, (in Fock basis)

    $$\begin{aligned} \ket{\psi_2} &= \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta\ket{\psi_0} \\ &\equiv [U_4]e^{-\tfrac{\theta'}{2}[n]_F}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[V][D][V^{-1}]}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[D]}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]\begin{bmatrix} e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_3} \end{bmatrix}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]\ket{\psi}_{\text{1}} \quad \leftarrow \text{rotated a little more toward } \ket{1} \\ &= e^{-i[\mathcal{H}_0]T/\hbar}\ket{\psi_1}, \quad [\mathcal{H}_0] \text{ is diagonal in the energy basis} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} \\ \beta e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} \\ \gamma e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}, \quad e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} = e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} \cdot e^{-iT(E_1 - E_0)/\hbar} = e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} \cdot e^{-i2\pi} = e^{-iTE_0/\hbar}\\ &= e^{-iTE_0/\hbar}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma e^{-iT(E_2 - E_0)/\hbar} \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{aligned}$$

    The global phase $e^{-iTE_0/\hbar}$ is unphysical. But the $\ket{2}$ component picks up an extra phase $e^{-iT(E_2 - E_0)/\hbar} \neq 1$, because $E_2 - E_0 = 2\hbar\omega_q + \alpha \neq 2\hbar\omega_q$.

    That residual phase on $\ket{2}$ is the anharmonicity doing its job — it rotates the leakage component relative to the qubit subspace, which is exactly what prevents the unbounded leakage accumulation we saw in the harmonic oscillator.

    The free evolution is no longer $\approx -I$ — it selectively phases the leakage level while leaving the computational subspace untouched.
