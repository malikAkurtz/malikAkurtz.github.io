  • Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

    This a summary of chapter 2.3 of Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, by Griffiths[1]

    The potential energy for a classical harmonic oscillator takes the form:

    $$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}kx^{2}$$

    Equivalently

    $$V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^{2}x^{2}, \quad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$

    The Time-Independent Schrodinger Equation (TISE) in the position basis reads:

    $$\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    and so we need to solve:

    $$\overbrace{\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2}{dx^2}}^{T}\psi(x) + \overbrace{\frac{m\omega^2 x^2}{2}}^{V(x)}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    Recall:

    $$T = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$$

    and $$p = mv$$ so

    $$T = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2} = \frac{m^{2}v^{2}}{2m} = \frac{p^{2}}{2m}$$

    TISE now becomes:

    $$\frac{p^2}{2m}\psi(x) + \frac{m\omega^2 x^2}{2}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    $$\iff$$

    $$\overbrace{\frac{1}{2m}\left(p^2 + (m\omega x)^2\right)}^{\mathcal{H}}\psi(x) = E\psi(x)$$

    So we wish to factor the Hamiltonian as:

    $$\mathcal{H} = \frac{1}{2m}\left(p^2 + (m\omega x)^2\right)$$

    Classically, this would be easy, we simply recall:

    $$(u^2 + v^2) = (u + iv)(u - iv)$$

    But we have to quantize and upgrade to operators first:

    $$\hat{\mathcal{H}} = \frac{1}{2m}\left(\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega \hat{x})^2\right)$$

    and operators do not commute, nonetheless, we can define:

    $$\hat{a}_{\pm} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\hbar m\omega}}\left(\mp i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}\right)$$

    Thus,

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}(i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x})(-i\hat{p} + m\omega\hat{x}) \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[(i\hat{p})(-i\hat{p}) + (i\hat{p})(m\omega\hat{x}) + (m\omega\hat{x})(-i\hat{p}) + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2 - im\omega(\hat{x}\hat{p} - \hat{p}\hat{x})\right] \quad \leftarrow \text{commutator} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] - \frac{i}{2\hbar}[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] \end{aligned}$$

    Now, we know that $$\hat{x}$$ and $$\hat{p}$$ are conjugate variables, so by definition,

    $$[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$$

    and we can now rewrite our equation as

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] - \frac{i}{2\hbar}(i\hbar) \\ &= \frac{1}{2\hbar m\omega}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right] + \frac{1}{2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\overbrace{\left(\frac{1}{2m}\left[\hat{p}^2 + (m\omega\hat{x})^2\right]\right)}^{\hat{\mathcal{H}}} + \frac{1}{2} \\ &= \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{\mathcal{H}} + \frac{1}{2} \quad \iff \quad \hat{\mathcal{H}} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_-\hat{a}_+ - \frac{1}{2}\right) \end{aligned}$$

    Similarly,

    $$\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- = \frac{1}{\hbar\omega}\hat{\mathcal{H}} - \frac{1}{2} \quad \iff \quad \hat{\mathcal{H}} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_+\hat{a}_- + \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

    Thus,

    $$\hat{\mathcal{H}} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_{\pm}\hat{a}_{\mp} \pm \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

    SE now reads:

    $$\hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}_{\pm}\hat{a}_{\mp} \pm \frac{1}{2}\right)\psi = E\psi$$

    $$\text{Lemma: } \hat{\mathcal{H}}\psi = E\psi \implies \hat{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{a}_+\psi) = (E + \hbar\omega)(\hat{a}_+\psi), \quad \hat{\mathcal{H}}(\hat{a}_-\psi) = (E - \hbar\omega)(\hat{a}_-\psi)$$

    $$\text{Lemma: } \hat{a}_+\psi_n = \sqrt{n+1}\;\psi_{n+1} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}_-\psi_n = \sqrt{n}\;\psi_{n-1}$$

    Also note,

    $$\hat{x} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}(\hat{a}_+ - \hat{a}_-) \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{p} = i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}(\hat{a}_+ - \hat{a}_-)$$

    Note:

    $$\hat{a}_+ = \hat{a}^\dagger, \quad \hat{a}_- = \hat{a}$$

    Zero-Point Fluctuations

    The zero point fluctuation of an arbitrary operator $\hat{A}$ is the standard deviation of $\hat{A}$ in state $\ket{0}$,

    $$\hat{A}_{\text{zpf}} = \sqrt{\bra{0}\hat{A}^2\ket{0} - (\bra{0}\hat{A}\ket{0})^2}$$

    Position Zero-Point Fluctuation

    $$\hat{x} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}(\hat{a}^{\dagger} + \hat{a})$$

    $$\begin{aligned} \implies \hat{x}|0\rangle &= \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}(\hat{a}^{\dagger}|0\rangle + \hat{a}|0\rangle) \\ &= \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}|1\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \langle 0|\hat{x}|0\rangle = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}\langle 0|1\rangle = 0$$

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{x}^2|0\rangle &= \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(\hat{a}^{\dagger} + \hat{a})^2|0\rangle \\ &= \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(\hat{a}^{\dagger 2} + \hat{a}^{\dagger}\hat{a} + \hat{a}\hat{a}^{\dagger} + \hat{a}^2)|0\rangle \\ &= \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(\sqrt{2}|2\rangle + 0 + |0\rangle + 0) \\ &= \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(|0\rangle + \sqrt{2}|2\rangle) \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \langle 0|\hat{x}^2|0\rangle = \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}$$

    $$\implies x_{\text{zpf}} = \sqrt{\langle 0|\hat{x}^2|0\rangle - \langle 0|\hat{x}|0\rangle^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}$$

    Momentum Zero-Point Fluctuation

    $$\hat{p} = i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}(\hat{a}^{\dagger} - \hat{a})$$

    $$\begin{aligned} \implies \hat{p}|0\rangle &= i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}(\hat{a}^{\dagger}|0\rangle - \hat{a}|0\rangle) \\ &= i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}|1\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \langle 0|\hat{p}|0\rangle = i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}\langle 0|1\rangle = 0$$

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{p}^2|0\rangle &= -\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}(\hat{a}^{\dagger} - \hat{a})^2|0\rangle \\ &= -\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}(\hat{a}^{\dagger 2} - \hat{a}^{\dagger}\hat{a} - \hat{a}\hat{a}^{\dagger} + \hat{a}^2)|0\rangle \\ &= -\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}(\sqrt{2}|2\rangle - 0 - |0\rangle + 0) \\ &= \frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}(|0\rangle - \sqrt{2}|2\rangle) \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \langle 0|\hat{p}^2|0\rangle = \frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}$$

    $$\implies p_{\text{zpf}} = \sqrt{\langle 0|\hat{p}^2|0\rangle - \langle 0|\hat{p}|0\rangle^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar m\omega}{2}}$$

    Thus:

    $$\hat{x} = x_{\text{zpf}}(\hat{a}^{\dagger} + \hat{a}), \qquad \hat{p} = ip_{\text{zpf}}(\hat{a}^{\dagger} - \hat{a})$$

    [1] D.J. Griffiths. Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, 3rd ed. Cambridge University Press, 2018.

  • The Fock Basis

    First, it is important to note

    $$\psi = \ket{\psi}$$

    this object is the physical wavefunction in the abstract sense, i.e. in no particular basis.

    We can then write

    $$\begin{aligned} |\psi\rangle &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle \\ &\equiv \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n|n\rangle, \quad |n\rangle = \vec{e}_n \\ &= |\psi\rangle \\ \end{aligned}$$

    Note the congruence $\equiv$ denoting the act of projection.

    Now, let us build $\hat{a}$ and $\hat{a}^{\dagger}$ in the Fock basis.

    We know:

    $$\hat{a}\,\psi_n = \sqrt{n}\;\psi_{n-1} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}^\dagger\psi_n = \sqrt{n+1}\;\psi_{n+1}$$

    Equivalently:

    $$\hat{a}\ket{n} = \sqrt{n}\ket{n-1} \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}^\dagger\ket{n} = \sqrt{n+1}\ket{n+1}$$

    $$\implies \bra{m}\hat{a}\ket{n} = \sqrt{n}\braket{m}{n-1} = \sqrt{n}\;\delta_{m,n-1}$$

    and

    $$\bra{m}\hat{a}^\dagger\ket{n} = \sqrt{n+1}\braket{m}{n+1} = \sqrt{n+1}\;\delta_{m,n+1}$$

    In the Fock basis,

    $$\hat{a} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \sqrt{1} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \sqrt{3} & \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \end{bmatrix}, \quad \hat{a}^\dagger \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ \sqrt{1} & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \sqrt{3} & \vdots & \end{bmatrix}$$

    $$\text{Now let } \hat{n} = \hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 3 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix} = [n]_F$$

    Thus,

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 &= \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) \\ &= \hbar\omega\left(\hat{n} + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) \\ &\equiv \hbar\omega\left([n]_F + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbb{I}\right) \end{aligned}$$

    Which is then diagonal in the Fock basis

    We can now diagonalize $\hat{\mathcal{H}_0}$ to get:

    $$[\mathcal{H}_0]_E = [V][\mathcal{H}_0]_F[V^{-1}] = [V][\mathcal{H}_0]_F[V^\dagger]$$

    Since $\hat{\mathcal{H_0}}$ is Hermitian $\implies V \text{ unitary} \implies V^{-1} = V^\dagger$

    and the columns of $[V]$ are the energy eigenstates/vectors of $[\mathcal{H}_0]_E$ in the Fock basis

    In the case of a harmonic oscillator, $[\mathcal{H}_0]_F$ is diagonal so $[V] = \mathbb{I}$

    i.e the energy basis and Fock basis are identical.

    [1] D.J. Griffiths. Introduction to Quantum Mechanics, 3rd ed. Cambridge University Press, 2018.

    These are notes on Compact Pulse Schedules for High-Fidelity Single-Flux Quantum Qubit Control, by Shillito et al.[1]

    Lets now set ncut = 3 and see how we might apply an RY gate

    Set our initial state

    $$\psi_0 = \ket{\psi_0} \equiv \sum_{n=1}^{3} c_n\ket{\psi_n} = \sum_{n=1}^{3} c_n\ket{n} = \vec{e}_1$$

    Define qubit period in seconds $T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$

    Define free evolution unitaries:

    $$\hat{U}(t)\ket{\psi} = \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 t/\hbar)\ket{\psi}$$

    let

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{U}_1 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_2 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{2T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_3 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{3T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_4 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{4T}{4})/\hbar) \end{aligned}$$

    Fix the kick angle defined by our system

    $$\theta = C_c \Phi_0 \sqrt{2\omega / C}, \quad \Phi_0 = \frac{h}{2e}$$

    w/ $C_c$ = capacitance between oscillator and SFQ driver

    Define the kick unitary operator

    $$\hat{U}_\theta = \exp(-\tfrac{\theta}{2}[\hat{a}^\dagger - \hat{a}])$$

    Then

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta) \approx \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta = \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta$$

    and

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{RY}(\theta_{\text{target}}) &\approx \hat{RY}(n\theta) \qquad (N = \theta_{\text{target}} / \theta) \\ &= \hat{RY}(\theta)^N \\ &\approx (\hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta)^N \\ &= (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^N \end{aligned}$$

    Let $\theta_{\text{target}} = \pi/2$ and $\theta = 0.03$ (typical) s.t $ = \frac{\pi}{2\theta} \approx 52$

    Also, let $C = m$ and $L = \tfrac{1}{k} s.t. $\omega = \sqrt{\tfrac{k}{m}} = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} rad/sec$

    So

    $$\hat{RY}(\tfrac{\pi}{2}) = (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^{52}$$

    Lets apply the first kick, (in Fock basis)

    $$\begin{aligned} \ket{\psi_2} &= \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta\ket{\psi_0} \\ &\equiv [U_4]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}([a^\dagger] - [a])}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[V][D][V^{-1}]}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[D]}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]\begin{bmatrix} e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_3} \end{bmatrix}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]\ket{\psi}_{\text{1}} \quad \leftarrow \text{rotated a little more toward } \ket{1} \\ &= e^{-i[\mathcal{H}_0]T/\hbar}\ket{\psi_1}, \quad [\mathcal{H}_0] \text{ is diagonal in the Fock basis} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iT\hbar\omega(0+\frac{1}{2})/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iT\hbar\omega(1+\frac{1}{2})/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iT\hbar\omega(2+\frac{1}{2})/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iT\omega/2} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-3iT\omega/2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-5iT\omega/2} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{\omega}\frac{\omega}{2}} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i\frac{3}{2}\frac{2\pi}{\omega}\omega} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-i\frac{5}{2}\frac{2\pi}{\omega}\omega} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-i\pi} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-3i\pi} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-5i\pi} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \\ &= -\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \quad \leftarrow \text{global phase} \end{aligned}$$

  • The Transmon, an Anharmonic Quantum Oscillator

    These are notes on Introduction to Quantum Electromagnetic Circuits, by Uri Vool and Michel Devoret

    Note that in a superconducting circuit,

    $$\text{Flux } \Phi \equiv \text{Position } x$$

    and

    $$\text{Charge } Q \equiv \text{Momentum } p$$

    in that

    $$[\hat{\Phi}, \hat{Q}] = i\hbar$$

    Also

    $$C \equiv \text{Mass } m$$

    Deriving the Potential Energy Term

    A transmon can be parameterized by a charging energy, $E_C$, as well as a ratio $\frac{E_J}{E_C} \gg 1$, where $E_J$ is the Josephson energy and

    $$E_C = \frac{e^2}{2C}$$

    The energy for the Josephson element in the phase basis reads:

    $$h_J(\Phi) = -E_J\cos\!\left(\frac{\Phi}{\Phi_0}\right), \quad \Phi_0 = \frac{\hbar}{2e}$$

    where $\varphi = \frac{\Phi}{\Phi_0}$ is the gauge-invariant phase difference, so equivalently:

    $$h_J(\varphi) = -E_J\cos(\varphi)$$

    In a superconducting circuit with a ground node electrode and an island electrode separated by a Josephson junction,

    $$n = \text{number of Cooper pairs that crossed the junction}$$

    and we know, in the charge basis:

    $$\hat{n} \equiv \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} n|n\rangle\langle n|$$

    Lemma: $\Delta n = \pm 1$

    Define $\hat{T}^+$ such that

    $$\hat{T}^+|n\rangle = |n+1\rangle$$

    Now,

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{T}^+ &= \hat{T}^+\mathbb{I} \\ &= \hat{T}^+\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n| \\ &= \sum_n \hat{T}^+|n\rangle\langle n| \\ &= \sum_n |n+1\rangle\langle n| \end{aligned}$$

    Then in the charge basis:

    $$\langle m|T^+|n\rangle = \langle m|n+1\rangle = \delta_{m,n+1}$$

    Similarly, define $\hat{T}^-$ such that

    $$\hat{T}^-|n\rangle = |n-1\rangle \iff \hat{T}^- = \sum_n |n-1\rangle\langle n|$$

    Then:

    $$\langle m|T^-|n\rangle = \delta_{m,n-1}$$

    Lemma: The coupling Hamiltonian reads:

    $$\hat{h}_{CPT} = -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[\hat{T}^- + \hat{T}^+\right]$$

    Now, in the charge basis, define the phase states:

    $$|\theta\rangle = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta}|n\rangle, \quad \theta \in [0, 2\pi)$$

    We want to show that:

    $$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta = |m\rangle$$

    So we expand,

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle &= \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta}|n\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    Integrate both sides:

    $$\begin{aligned} \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta &= \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-im\theta}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta}|n\rangle\,d\theta \\ &= \sum_n |n\rangle \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta \end{aligned}$$

    Case 1: $n = m$

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot 2\pi \\ &= 1 \end{aligned}$$

    Case 2: $n \neq m$

    $$\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta = 0$$

    Therefore:

    $$\begin{aligned} \sum_n |n\rangle \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(n-m)\theta}\,d\theta &= \sum_n |n\rangle\,\delta_{n,m} \\ &= |m\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    $$\implies \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{-im\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta = |m\rangle$$

    Now rewrite $\hat{h}_{CPT}$:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{h}_{CPT} &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[\hat{T}^- + \hat{T}^+\right] \\ &\equiv -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \left[|n-1\rangle\langle n| + |n+1\rangle\langle n|\right] \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \left[|n-1\rangle + |n+1\rangle\right]\langle n| \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \left[\left(\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-i(n-1)\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta\right) + \left(\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-i(n+1)\theta}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta\right)\right]\left(\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}e^{in\theta}\langle\theta|\,d\theta\right) \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\left(e^{-i(n-1)\theta} + e^{-i(n+1)\theta}\right)e^{in\theta}|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|\,d\theta \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\sum_n \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2\pi}\left(e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}\right)|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|\,d\theta \end{aligned}$$

    Now define the phase operator:

    $$e^{i\hat{\theta}} \equiv \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\theta}|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|\,d\theta$$

    Then:

    $$e^{i\hat{\theta}}|n\rangle \equiv \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\theta}|\theta\rangle\langle\theta|n\rangle\,d\theta$$

    Using

    $$|\theta\rangle = \sum_n e^{in\theta}|n\rangle \iff \langle\theta| = \sum_m e^{-im\theta}\langle m|$$

    we get

    $$\langle\theta|n\rangle = e^{-in\theta}$$

    so:

    $$\begin{aligned} e^{i\hat{\theta}}|n\rangle &\equiv \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{i\theta}|\theta\rangle e^{-in\theta}\,d\theta \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} e^{-i\theta(n-1)}|\theta\rangle\,d\theta \\ &= |n-1\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    Thus:

    $$e^{i\hat{\theta}} = \hat{T}^-$$

    Similarly:

    $$e^{-i\hat{\theta}} = \hat{T}^+$$

    And so:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{h}_{CPT} &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[\hat{T}^- + \hat{T}^+\right] \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\left[e^{i\hat{\theta}} + e^{-i\hat{\theta}}\right] \\ &= -\frac{E_J}{2}\cdot 2\cos(\hat{\theta}) \\ &= -E_J\cos(\hat{\theta}) \end{aligned}$$

    Let $\varphi \mod 2\pi = \theta, \quad \varphi \in \mathbb{R}, \quad \theta \in [0, 2\pi)$, then:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{h}_J &\equiv h_J(\varphi) \\ &= -E_J\cos(\varphi) \\ &= -E_J\cos(\theta) \\ &\equiv -E_J\cos(\hat{\theta}) \\ &= \hat{h}_{CPT} \end{aligned}$$

    Also note that since $\theta$ is periodic, only periodic functions of $\hat{\theta}$, like $e^{i\hat{\theta}}$ are unambiguous

    This confirms that the Josephson cosine potential and the Cooper pair tunneling Hamiltonian are identical.

    All this to say, the potential energy for a transmon takes the form:

    $$V(\varphi) = h_J(\varphi) = h_{CPT}(\varphi) = -E_J\cos(\varphi)$$

    In operator form:

    $$\hat{V} = -E_J\cos(\hat{\varphi})$$

    Deriving the Potential Energy Term

    Fairly simple,

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{T} &= \tfrac{\hat{Q}^2}{2C}, \quad \hat{Q} = 2e\hat{n} \\ &= \tfrac{(2e\hat{n})^2}{2C} \\ &= \tfrac{4e^2\hat{n}^2}{2C} \\ &= 4E_C\hat{n}^2 \end{aligned}$$

    That is,

    $$\hat{T} = 4E_C\hat{n}^2$$

    The full transmon Hamiltonian is therefore:

    $$\hat{H} = 4E_C\hat{n}^2 - E_J\cos(\hat{\varphi})$$

    and since $\hat{n}$ is diagonal in the charge basis, we get

    $$\hat{H} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \\ \cdots & 16E_C & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ \cdots & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 4E_C & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ \cdots & 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 0 & \cdots \\ \cdots & 0 & 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 4E_C & -\frac{E_J}{2} & \cdots \\ \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 & -\frac{E_J}{2} & 16E_C & \cdots \\ & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix} = [\hat{\mathcal{H}}]_{charge}$$

    Now diagonalize $\hat{H}$ in the charge basis:

    $$[\mathcal{H}]_{\text{charge}} = [V][\mathcal{H}]_E[V^\dagger]$$

    where $[\mathcal{H}]_E = \text{diag}(E_0, E_1, E_2, \ldots)$ and the columns of $[V]$ are the energy eigenstates in the charge basis.

    Define the anharmonicity:

    $$\alpha = (E_2 - E_1) - (E_1 - E_0)$$

    the qubit frequency:

    $$f_q = \frac{E_1 - E_0}{h}$$

    and the angular frequency:

    $$\omega_q = 2\pi f_q$$

    Note: unlike the harmonic oscillator, $[\mathcal{H}]_{\text{charge}}$ is not diagonal, so $[V] \neq \mathbb{I}$ — the energy basis and charge basis are distinct.

    Now the quantum constitutive relations:

    Recall

    $$[\hat{\Phi}, \hat{Q}] = i\hbar$$

    Now, since $\hat{\varphi} = \hat{\Phi}/\Phi_0$, $\Phi_0 = \hbar/2e$:

    $$\begin{aligned} [\hat{\Phi}, \hat{Q}] &= i\hbar \\ &\iff \\ [\hat{\Phi}/\Phi_0, \hat{Q}] &= i\hbar/\Phi_0 \\ &\iff \\ [\hat{\varphi}, \hat{Q}] &= i\hbar\left(\frac{2e}{\hbar}\right) \\ &\iff \\ [\hat{\varphi}, \hat{Q}] &= 2ei, \quad \hat{Q} = 2e\hat{n} \\ &\iff \\ [\hat{\varphi}, 2e\hat{n}] &= 2ei \\ &\iff \\ [\hat{\varphi}, \hat{n}] &= i \\ &\iff \\ [\hat{\theta}, \hat{n}] &= i \end{aligned}$$

    Using eq. 3.7 in the paper:

    $$\frac{\partial\hat{\theta}}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat{\theta}, \hat{\mathcal{H}}]$$

    and eq. 3.6:

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial\hat{\mathcal{H}}}{\partial\hat{n}} &= \frac{-1}{i}[\hat{\mathcal{H}}, \hat{\theta}] \\ &\iff \\ \frac{\partial\hat{\mathcal{H}}}{\partial\hat{n}} &= \frac{1}{i}[\hat{\theta}, \hat{\mathcal{H}}] \end{aligned}$$

    So:

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{d\hat{\theta}}{dt} &= \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat{\theta}, \hat{\mathcal{H}}] \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\frac{\partial\hat{\mathcal{H}}}{\partial\hat{n}} \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\frac{\partial}{\partial\hat{n}}\left[4E_C\hat{n}^2 - E_J\cos\hat{\theta}\right] \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\frac{\partial}{\partial\hat{n}}\left[4\left(\frac{e^2}{2C}\right)\hat{n}^2 - E_J\cos\hat{\theta}\right] \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\cdot 8\left(\frac{e^2}{2C}\right)\hat{n} \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\cdot\frac{4e^2}{2C}\cdot(2\hat{n}) \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\cdot\frac{2e\cdot 2e}{C}\cdot\hat{n} \\[8pt] &= \frac{1}{\hbar}\cdot\frac{2e}{C}\cdot\hat{Q} \\[8pt] &= \frac{2e}{\hbar}\cdot\frac{\hat{Q}}{C} \\[8pt] &= \frac{2e}{\hbar}\hat{v} \end{aligned}$$

    And so finally:

    $$\begin{aligned} \frac{d\hat{\theta}}{dt} &= \frac{2e}{\hbar}\hat{v} \\ &\iff \\ \frac{d\hat{\varphi}}{dt} &= \frac{2e}{\hbar}\hat{v} \end{aligned}$$

    Similarly:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{i} &= 2e\frac{d\hat{n}}{dt} \\ &\iff \\ \hat{i} &= I_0\sin\hat{\varphi}, \quad I_0 = \frac{2e}{\hbar}E_J \end{aligned}$$

    Also note that $\hat{H}$ can be approximated as follows:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{H} &\approx \hbar \omega_q\hat{b}^{\dagger}\hat{b} - \tfrac{\alpha}{2}\hat{b}^{\dagger 2}\hat{b}^2 \\ &\equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & & & \\ & \hbar\omega_q & & \\ & & 2\hbar\omega_q - \alpha & \\ & & & 3\hbar\omega_q - 3\alpha & \\ & & & & \ddots \end{bmatrix} \\ &= [\hat{\tilde{H}}]_F \end{aligned}$$

    Similarly, $\hat{n}$ can be approximated as:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{n} &\approx ir(\hat{b}^{\dagger} - \hat{b}) \\ &\equiv \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -ir & 0 & \cdots \\ ir & 0 & -i\sqrt{2}\,r & \cdots \\ 0 & i\sqrt{2}\,r & 0 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix} \\ &= [\hat{\tilde{n}}]_F, \quad r = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{E_J/E_C}{2}\right)^{1/4} \end{aligned}$$

    We will use the Fock approximated $\hat{n}$ to build the kick unitary $\hat{U}_{\theta}$, as Shillito does in the paper.[1]

    Lets once more set ncut = 3 and see how we might apply an RY gate

    Set our initial state

    Define free evolution unitaries:

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{U}_1 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_2 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{2T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_3 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{3T}{4})/\hbar) \\ \hat{U}_4 &= \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0(\tfrac{4T}{4})/\hbar) \end{aligned}$$

    Fix the kick angle defined by our system

    $$\theta = C_c \Phi_0 \sqrt{2\omega_q / C}$$

    w/ $C_c$ = capacitance between oscillator and SFQ driver

    Define the kick unitary operator

    $$\hat{U}_\theta = \exp(\tfrac{\theta'}{2}\hat{n})$$

    where

    $$\theta' = \theta / r$$

    Then

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta) \approx \exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta = \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta$$

    and

    $$\begin{aligned} \hat{RY}(\theta_{\text{target}}) &\approx \hat{RY}(N\theta) \qquad (N = \theta_{\text{target}} / \theta) \\ &= \hat{RY}(\theta)^N \\ &\approx (\hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta)^N \\ &= (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^N \end{aligned}$$

    Let $\theta_{\text{target}} = \pi/2$ and $\theta = 0.03$ (typical) s.t $N = \frac{\pi}{2\theta} \approx 52$

    So

    $$\hat{RY}(\tfrac{\pi}{2}) = (\exp(-i\hat{\mathcal{H}}_0 T)\hat{U}_\theta)^{52}$$

    Lets apply the first kick, (in Fock basis)

    $$\begin{aligned} \ket{\psi_2} &= \hat{U}_4\hat{U}_\theta\ket{\psi_0} \\ &\equiv [U_4]e^{-\tfrac{\theta'}{2}[n]_F}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[V][D][V^{-1}]}\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}[D]}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4][V]\begin{bmatrix} e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_1} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}\lambda_3} \end{bmatrix}[V^{-1}]\vec{e}_1 \\ &= [U_4]\ket{\psi}_{\text{1}} \quad \leftarrow \text{rotated a little more toward } \ket{1} \\ &= e^{-i[\mathcal{H}_0]T/\hbar}\ket{\psi_1}, \quad [\mathcal{H}_0] \text{ is diagonal in the energy basis} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\ket{\psi_1} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} \\ \beta e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} \\ \gamma e^{-iTE_2/\hbar} \end{bmatrix}, \quad e^{-iTE_1/\hbar} = e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} \cdot e^{-iT(E_1 - E_0)/\hbar} = e^{-iTE_0/\hbar} \cdot e^{-i2\pi} = e^{-iTE_0/\hbar}\\ &= e^{-iTE_0/\hbar}\begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \\ \gamma e^{-iT(E_2 - E_0)/\hbar} \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{aligned}$$

    The global phase $e^{-iTE_0/\hbar}$ is unphysical. But the $\ket{2}$ component picks up an extra phase $e^{-iT(E_2 - E_0)/\hbar} \neq 1$, because $E_2 - E_0 = 2\hbar\omega_q + \alpha \neq 2\hbar\omega_q$.

    That residual phase on $\ket{2}$ is the anharmonicity doing its job — it rotates the leakage component relative to the qubit subspace, which is exactly what prevents the unbounded leakage accumulation we saw in the harmonic oscillator.

    The free evolution is no longer $\approx -I$ — it selectively phases the leakage level while leaving the computational subspace untouched.

  • Encoding the Pulse Sequence

    These are notes on Compact Pulse Schedules for High-Fidelity Single-Flux Quantum Qubit Control, by Shillito et al.[1]

    The Kick Operator

    The SFQ kick applied to the qubit can be represented as a displacement operator:

    $$\hat{U}_\theta = \exp\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}[\hat{b}^\dagger - \hat{b}]\right), \quad \theta = C_c \Phi_0\sqrt{\frac{2\omega_q}{C}}$$

    where $C_c$ is the coupling capacitance and $\Phi_0 = h/2e$ is the flux quantum. Typically $\theta \approx 0.03$ rad.

    Building Gates from Kicks

    Unitary operations on the qubit are modeled as a series of $N$ kicks interleaved with free evolution:

    $$\hat{U} = \prod_{i=0}^{N} \exp\!\left(\frac{-i\hat{H}t_i}{\hbar}\right)\hat{U}_\theta$$

    where $t_i$ is the free-evolution time between kick $i$ and kick $i+1$.

    In the two-level approximation, a single kick followed by one full qubit cycle of free evolution approximates an $RY$ rotation:

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta) \approx \exp\!\left(\frac{-i\hat{H}T_q}{\hbar}\right)\hat{U}_\theta, \quad T_q = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_q}$$

    Chaining $N$ such kick-evolve pairs gives an arbitrary rotation:

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta_{\text{target}}) \approx \hat{RY}(\theta)^N, \quad N = \frac{\theta_{\text{target}}}{\theta}$$

    SFQ-DRAG Decomposition

    The SFQ analog of DRAG decomposes any stand-alone pulse train into a ramp-on, train, and ramp-off segment:

    $$\hat{U} = \hat{U}(t_r)_{\text{on}}\,\hat{U}_{\text{train}}\,\hat{U}(t_r)_{\text{off}}, \quad t_r = \text{ramp time}$$

    e.g. $\hat{U}_{\text{train}} = \hat{RY}(\theta)^N$.

    Bit-String Encoding

    Given an SFQ clock frequency $f_c$ with period $T_c = 1/f_c$, define

    $$M = \frac{T_q}{T_c}, \quad T_q \gg T_c$$

    so $M$ is the number of clock cycles per qubit cycle. Each qubit cycle is then described by a bit string

    $$s = (s_0,\, s_1,\, \ldots,\, s_M) \in \{0,1\}^{M-1} $$

    with the convention:

    $$s_i = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{free-evolve the qubit for } T_c \text{ seconds}$$

    $$s_i = 1 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{apply an instantaneous kick, then free-evolve for } T_c \text{ seconds}$$

    $$\begin{aligned} 1000 \implies &\; e^{-i\hat{H}T_c/\hbar} \, e^{-i\hat{H}T_c/\hbar} \, e^{-i\hat{H}T_c/\hbar} \, e^{-i\hat{H}T_c/\hbar} \, \hat{U}_\theta \\ &= e^{4(-i\hat{H}T_c/\hbar)} \, \hat{U}_\theta \\ &= e^{-i\hat{H}4T_c/\hbar} \, \hat{U}_\theta \\ &= e^{-i\hat{H}T_q/\hbar} \, \hat{U}_\theta \end{aligned}$$

    This encodes a single-kick gate: one kick at the start, followed by $M=4$ free clock cycles totalling one full qubit period.

    It is imperative that $\hat{U}_{\text{on}}$ and $\hat{U}_{\text{off}}$ destructively interfere depending on the gate being approximated by the SFQ pulse train. That is to say "The off-ramp pulses $\hat{U}_{\text{off}}$ should be symmetric with respect to the on-ramp unitary along the Y-axis and anti-symmetric along the X-axis for the implementation of an $\hat{RY}(\theta)$ gate."

    In order to do this, we first decompose our on-ramp unitary as:

    $$\hat{U}_{on}(t_r) = c_I\hat{I} + c_x\hat{\sigma}_x + c_y\hat{\sigma}_y + c_z\hat{\sigma}_z$$

    Now, if we are effectuating an $\hat{RY}(\theta)$ gate, we would have:

    $$\hat{U}_{off}(t_r) = c_I\hat{I} - c_x\hat{\sigma}_x + c_y\hat{\sigma}_y + c_z\hat{\sigma}_z$$

    To remove any accumulated rotation along the X-axis, similarly, if we are effectuating an $\hat{RX}(\theta)$ gate, we would have:

    $$\hat{U}_{off}(t_r) = c_I\hat{I} + c_x\hat{\sigma}_x - c_y\hat{\sigma}_y + c_z\hat{\sigma}_z$$

    To remove any accumulated rotation along the Y-axis.

  • Transmon Free Evolution

    These are notes on Compact Pulse Schedules for High-Fidelity Single-Flux Quantum Qubit Control, by Shillito et al.[1]

    Full Free Evolution Unitary

    The free evolution unitary is:

    $$\hat{U}_f(t) = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar} \equiv e^{-i[\mathcal{H}]_E t/\hbar}$$

    Since $[\mathcal{H}]_E$ is diagonal in the energy basis, the matrix exponential is just the exponential of each diagonal entry:

    $$= \begin{bmatrix} e^{-iE_0 t/\hbar} & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & e^{-iE_1 t/\hbar} & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-iE_2 t/\hbar} & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix}$$

    Factor out $e^{-iE_0 t/\hbar}$ as a global phase, using $\frac{-iE_n t}{\hbar} + \frac{iE_0 t}{\hbar} = \frac{-i(E_n-E_0)t}{\hbar}$:

    $$= e^{-iE_0 t/\hbar} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & e^{-i(E_1-E_0)t/\hbar} & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & e^{-i(E_2-E_0)t/\hbar} & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \end{bmatrix}$$

    Using the transmon energy differences $E_n - E_0 = n\omega_q - \frac{\alpha}{2\hbar}n(n-1)$:

    $$\hat{U}_f(t) \equiv e^{-iE_0 t/\hbar} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & & & \\ & e^{-i\omega_q t} & & \\ & & e^{-i(2\omega_q - \alpha/\hbar)t} & \\ & & & e^{-i\left(n\omega_q - \frac{\alpha}{2\hbar}n(n-1)\right)t} \end{bmatrix}$$

    Reduction to $RZ$ in the Qubit Subspace

    Since $e^{-iE_0 t/\hbar}I$ was already dropped as a global phase on the full $n_\text{cut}$-level space, the free evolution unitary is:

    $$\hat{U}_f(t) \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 1 & & & \\ & e^{-i\omega_q t} & & \\ & & e^{-i(2\omega_q - \alpha/\hbar)t} & \\ & & & \ddots \end{bmatrix}$$

    Projecting onto $\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\}$:

    $$\hat{U}_f(t)\big|_{\text{qubit}} \equiv \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i\omega_q t} \end{bmatrix}$$

    No further phase removal is needed — the only global phase has already been removed on the full space before projection.

    Recall:

    $$RZ(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{-i\theta/2} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\theta/2} \end{bmatrix} \in SU(2)$$

    In the no-leakage limit, the qubit subspace is dynamically closed, so $e^{-i\theta/2}I_{2\times 2}$ is a valid global phase and can be factored out:

    $$RZ(\theta) = e^{-i\theta/2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\theta} \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{drop global phase}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\theta} \end{bmatrix}$$

    Matching with the projected free evolution unitary: $e^{i\theta} = e^{-i\omega_q t} \implies \theta = -\omega_q t$, so:

    $$\hat{U}_f(t)\big|_{\text{qubit}} = \hat{RZ}(-\omega_q t) \equiv RZ(-\omega_q t) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\theta} \end{bmatrix}$$

    Free evolution in the qubit subspace is precisely an $RZ$ rotation accumulating phase at rate $\omega_q$. This identification is exact only in the no-leakage limit where the qubit subspace is dynamically closed. In the case where leakage is present (real-world), we only have an approximation:

    $$\hat{U}_f(t)\big|_{\text{qubit}} \approx \hat{RZ}(-\omega_q t) \equiv RZ(-\omega_q t)$$

  • Bloch Sphere Parameterization

    A general qubit state can be written as:

    $$|\psi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}\alpha\\\beta\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}a+ib\\c+id\end{pmatrix}$$

    Converting each component to polar form $re^{i\theta}$:

    $$= \begin{pmatrix}\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\;e^{i\,\mathrm{atan2}(b,a)}\\\sqrt{c^2+d^2}\;e^{i\,\mathrm{atan2}(d,c)}\end{pmatrix} = |\alpha|e^{i\tau}|0\rangle + |\beta|e^{i\varphi}|1\rangle$$

    Eliminating the Global Phase

    Factor out $e^{i\tau}$ (global phase, physically irrelevant):

    $$= e^{i\tau}\!\left(|\alpha||0\rangle + |\beta|e^{i(\varphi-\tau)}|1\rangle\right) \equiv |\alpha||0\rangle + e^{i\phi}|\beta||1\rangle, \quad \phi = \varphi - \tau$$

    This leaves 3 free real parameters: $|\alpha|$, $|\beta|$, and $\phi$.

    Note that $\phi$ is precisely the relative phase between $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}.$

    Enforcing Normalization

    The constraint $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$ is satisfied by writing

    $$\begin{aligned} |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 &= 1 \\ &= \cos^2\tfrac{\theta}{2} + \sin^2\tfrac{\theta}{2} \\ &\implies |\alpha| = \cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}, \quad |\beta| = \sin\tfrac{\theta}{2} \\ &\implies |\alpha||0\rangle + e^{i\phi}|\beta||1\rangle = \cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}|0\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}|1\rangle \end{aligned}$$

    Hence leaving us with two real parameters, $\theta \in [0, \pi], \quad \phi \in [0, 2\pi)$, where the inclination (polar angle) is:

    $$\begin{aligned} \theta &= 2\arccos(\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}) \\ &= 2\arcsin(\sqrt{c^2 + d^2}) \end{aligned}$$

    and the azimuthal angle is:

    $$\begin{aligned} \phi &= \varphi - \tau \\ &= \operatorname{atan2}(d, c) - \operatorname{atan2}(b, a) \end{aligned}$$

    $$|\psi\rangle = \cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}|0\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}|1\rangle$$

    1. Pauli-X Expectation Value

    $$\begin{aligned} \langle X \rangle &= \langle \psi | X | \psi \rangle \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \beta \\ \alpha \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \alpha^* \beta + \beta^* \alpha \\ &= (a - bi)(c + di) + (c - di)(a + bi) \\ &= (ac + adi - bci + bd) + (ca + cbi - dai + db) \\ &= 2ac + 2bd = 2(ac + bd) \\ &= 2\text{Re}(\alpha^* \beta) \\ &= 2\text{Re}(|\alpha|e^{-i\tau}|\beta|e^{i\varphi}) \\ &= 2|\alpha||\beta|\text{Re}(e^{i(\varphi - \tau)}) \\ &= 2|\alpha||\beta|\text{Re}(\cos\phi + i\sin\phi) \\ &= 2|\alpha||\beta|\cos\phi \\ &= 2\cos\frac{\theta}{2}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\cos\phi \\ &= \sin\theta\cos\phi = x \end{aligned}$$

    2. Pauli-Y Expectation Value

    $$\begin{aligned} \langle Y \rangle &= \langle \psi | Y | \psi \rangle \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -i\beta \\ i\alpha \end{bmatrix} \\ &= -i\alpha^*\beta + i\beta^*\alpha \\ &= -i[(a - bi)(c + di)] + i[(c - di)(a + bi)] \\ &= -i[2adi - 2bci] = 2ad - 2bc \\ &= 2(ad - bc) \\ &= 2\text{Im}(\alpha^* \beta) \\ &= 2\text{Im}(|\alpha|e^{-i\tau}|\beta|e^{i\varphi}) \\ &= 2|\alpha||\beta|\text{Im}(e^{i\phi}) \\ &= 2\cos\frac{\theta}{2}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\sin\phi \\ &= \sin\theta\sin\phi = y \end{aligned}$$

    3. Pauli-Z Expectation Value

    $$\begin{aligned} \langle Z \rangle &= \langle \psi | Z | \psi \rangle \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \\ -\beta \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \alpha^*\alpha - \beta^*\beta \\ &= |\alpha|^2 - |\beta|^2 \\ &= \cos^2\frac{\theta}{2} - \sin^2\frac{\theta}{2} \\ &= \cos 2\cdot\frac{\theta}{2} \\ &= \cos\theta = z \end{aligned}$$

    Thus, our rectangular coordinates are precisely the expectation values of each Pauli operator,

    $$\begin{aligned} x &= \sin\theta\cos\phi = \langle X \rangle\\ y &= \sin\theta\sin\phi = \langle Y \rangle \\ z &= \cos\theta = \langle Z \rangle \end{aligned}$$

  • The Unitary Group & SU(2)

    The unitary group is defined as:

    $$U(2) = \{U \in \mathbb{C}^{2\times 2} \mid U^\dagger U = UU^\dagger = I\}$$

    The special unitary group is the subgroup:

    $$\begin{aligned} SU(2) &= \left\{U = \begin{bmatrix}\alpha & -\bar{\beta} \\ \beta & \bar{\alpha}\end{bmatrix} \;\middle|\; \alpha,\beta \in \mathbb{C},\; \det(U) = |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\right\} \\ &\subseteq U(2) \end{aligned}$$

    Also, for every $U \in U(2)$, there exists $V \in SU(2)$ such that $U = e^{i\phi}V$. That is to say:

    $$SU(2) \cong U(2)/U(1)$$

    where

    $$U(1) = \{e^{i\phi}I \mid \phi \in [0, 2\pi)\}$$

    Generators of SU(2)

    The generators are the identity and the three Pauli matrices:

    $$I = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 1\end{bmatrix}, \quad \sigma_x = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1\\1 & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \sigma_y = \begin{bmatrix}0 & -i\\i & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \sigma_z = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & -1\end{bmatrix}$$

    Decomposition Theorem

    For every $U \in SU(2)$, there exists $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ and a unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = (n_x, n_y, n_z) \in S^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ such that:

    $$\begin{aligned} U &= \exp\!\left(-i\frac{\theta}{2}(n_x\sigma_x + n_y\sigma_y + n_z\sigma_z)\right) \\ &= \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\, I - i\sin\frac{\theta}{2}(n_x\sigma_x + n_y\sigma_y + n_z\sigma_z) \end{aligned}$$

    where $S^2 = \{\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid \|\mathbf{v}\| = 1\}$ is the unit 2-sphere.

    Thus,

    $$\hat{RX}(\theta) = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\,\hat{I} - i\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\,\hat{\sigma}_x \equiv \begin{bmatrix}\cos\frac{\theta}{2} & -i\sin\frac{\theta}{2} \\ -i\sin\frac{\theta}{2} & \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\end{bmatrix}$$

    $$\hat{RY}(\theta) = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\,\hat{I} - i\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\,\hat{\sigma}_y \equiv \begin{bmatrix}\cos\frac{\theta}{2} & -\sin\frac{\theta}{2} \\ \sin\frac{\theta}{2} & \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\end{bmatrix}$$

    $$\hat{RZ}(\theta) = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\,\hat{I} - i\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\,\hat{\sigma}_z \equiv \begin{bmatrix} e^{-i\frac{\theta}{2}} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{i\frac{\theta}{2}} \end{bmatrix}$$
