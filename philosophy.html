    Time spent deciding how to best use one's time itself uses time. So one must answer the question: How much time should be allocated to figuring out what do to with one's time? It seems as though this a problem that only curses the priveleged, that is, those with the freedom to choose what they want to spend their "free time" on. One can compare their human needs to a glass of water. A "priveleged" individual would then be one whose glass is overflowing with excess time, which now burdens them with a new problem consisting of what they should do with that time. An underpriveleged individual is only burdened with the problem of filling their glass, and that objective uses up all their time, thus they have none left over to be bothered figuring out what to do with the rest of it. All this to say, Philosophy is a practice reserved for the priveleged, the cursed, for only those with excess time have the resources that free them from the sole objective of survival, and onto the second objective of deciphering why it is even worth surviving.
